# Phase 2 — Market & Comparable Research

**Prerequisite:** Phase 1 synthesis in hand. This phase looks *outward*.

**Dual mandate (product owner decision, locked):** don't just catalogue what comparable products do — explain **why they've chosen the lane they play in**. The output feeds both UX simplification *and* PayOps's one-line positioning.

## The interrogation (answer these FIRST — before cataloguing anything)

Do not proceed to a landscape table until these are answered with evidence and citations. A pretty landscape that dodges these is a failed Phase 2.

1. **Product or feature?** Is what we're building sold as a standalone, paid product anywhere — or does it only ever exist embedded inside a bigger platform? Find the standalone examples (or prove there are none).
2. **Why would anyone pay?** Quantify the pain (real numbers, cited). If you can't find the pain in money or time lost, say so — that's a red flag about the product itself.
3. **Does it add friction or remove it?** Be honest: does injecting this step pull the user *out* of an existing flow (a detour), or does it *connect/replace* fragmented steps they already suffer through? A detour is a nice-to-have; a connector is a product.
4. **What do the incumbents do right that we don't — and what can we do that they structurally can't?** Name the structural reason we get to exist (e.g. independence, no-migration, separation of duties), not a feature list.
5. **Willingness to pay / monetisation reality.** How do incumbents make money, and can we make money the same way? If not, what must be true for our model to work?

If the honest answer is "this is really a feature," say that plainly and recommend the strongest framing anyway. Do not rescue a weak thesis.

## Research targets

- **Adjacent B2B fintech ops tools** — spend/AP automation, vendor verification/KYB, approval-workflow and treasury-lite products (global and Nigeria/Africa-specific).
- **The control primitives** — how mature products present four-eyes/maker-checker, verification gates, exception handling, and audit trails *simply*.
- **Design references PayOps already aspires to** — Stripe Dashboard, Linear (per `PRODUCT.md`).

## For each comparable, capture

- **Who it's for and the one job it leads with** — its spine.
- **Why it plays there** — the wedge, the buyer, the trust story.
- **What it makes simple that PayOps makes complex** — concrete, borrowable UX moves.
- **What PayOps does that it doesn't** — genuine differentiation to protect.

## Output

- **Landscape map** — where PayOps sits; who occupies the neighbouring lanes and why.
- **Borrowable patterns** — ranked list of specific UX simplifications, each tied to a Phase 1 finding and a comparable that proves it.
- **Positioning options** — 2–3 candidate one-line positions for PayOps, each with the "why this lane" rationale and the trade-off it accepts.
- **Cite every source** (per org policy). No invented market facts — if a claim can't be sourced, label it as inference.

Feeds Phase 3 (which patterns to adopt) and Phase 4 (the positioning line).
